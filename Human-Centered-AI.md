Developing AI according to the Human-Centered AI (HCAI) framework fundamentally shifts the design focus from creating purely autonomous machines to building systems that offer **both high levels of human control and high levels of computer automation**. This approach leans on an empiricist philosophy that prioritizes meaningful human control, empathy for user needs, and the design of technologies that amplify, augment, empower, and enhance human performance. 

The core design implications of this approach include:

**Designing AI as "Supertools" rather than "Teammates"**
Instead of designing computers to mimic humans with emotions or act as autonomous teammates, designers should focus on creating powerful "supertools," tele-bots, and active appliances. These tools should maximize a user's initiative, creativity, and responsibility, allowing them to carry out complex tasks with confidence and mastery. 

**Ensuring Predictability, Comprehensibility, and Control**
A primary implication is that humans must be able to anticipate what an AI system will do next, because humans ultimately bear the responsibility for the outcomes. To achieve this, designers should:
*   **Provide informative feedback:** Users need to know that their intent is being carried out and must be given enough time to stop or change an action, such as through the inclusion of "Cancel" buttons.
*   **Utilize direct manipulation:** Objects and actions should be clearly represented on the screen so that users can visually guide the process, such as dropping a file into a visible trash can or using touch screen pinches and swipes.
*   **Implement "Preview First" mechanics:** For temporal sequences or robot operations, users should be able to preview an entire process, select their plan, initiate the activity, and then manage its execution (similar to how digital cameras or navigation tools work).

**Adopting Established User Interface Guidelines**
HCAI development should rely on the **Eight Golden Rules** of interface design, which include striving for consistency, seeking universal usability, designing dialogs to yield closure, preventing errors, permitting easy reversal of actions, and reducing short-term memory load. 

**Applying the HCAI Pattern Language**
Designers are encouraged to follow a specific pattern language to resolve common design problems in AI:
*   **Visual Exploration:** Use the mantra "Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand" to help users navigate massive datasets.
*   **Interactive Steering:** Enable users to control processes through interactive control panels, including joysticks, sliders, or augmented/virtual reality interfaces.
*   **Collaboration:** Facilitate human-to-human communication, keeping "humans in the group; computers in the loop".

**Building in Safety, Accountability, and Auditability**
To guarantee that AI systems remain reliable, safe, and trustworthy—especially in life-critical and consequential applications—designers must anticipate failures. The design implications for safety include:
*   **Audit Trails:** Systems should be equipped with activity logs or "flight data recorders" that capture history from sensors. This signals humility in design and enables forensic, retrospective analysis of failures or near misses.
*   **Incident Reporting:** Incorporating feedback loops and incident reporting systems allows developers to continuously refine and improve their technology products based on real-world usage. 
*   **Interlocks and Guards:** Designers should include formal constraints and interlocks to prevent human mistakes and ensure algorithms only produce acceptable outcomes, avoiding the dangers of excessive human control or excessive computer automation.
